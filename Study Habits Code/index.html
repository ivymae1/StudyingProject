How to navigate the tricky world of college studying and make it out with good grades

Synopsis

Study habits can make or break how well a student does in class. College is a different ballpark than high school, and discovering what habits and skills work for you can be difficult. Initially, my research focused on how students are handling large lecture classes, as these are usually what students say gives them the most trouble. We interviewed 3 different students from various majors and with very different personalities. From these interviews, one of the consistencies was that all three agreed a smaller, intimate classroom setting was more beneficial for their learning. Their reasoning behind this was that they had more access to teacher’s help, they could freely ask questions, and they could actually get to know your peers. All of those things are hard to get in a lecture hall. Another answer that all three students agreed on was that having a friend, or just being able to talk over concepts with a peer can immensely help how well you do in a class. These answers were consistent with the research I conducted when writing my paper. Most sources agree that smaller classes are more beneficial for learning, focus and creating a comfortable space where students aren’t afraid to share their ideas. Smaller classes are typical in high school, but if you attend a public college the majority of courses are large lecture style. This makes sense as it saves the school money and can teach hundreds of students at a time, but it doesn’t benefit the student. One way to make these classes better is to form study groups with your peers. Research showed that despite previous grades/ learning abilities, those who participated in outside study groups did significantly better in that class than those who did not. On top of this, forming an outside group can give students a small classroom feel, and help form friendships and connections outside of the classroom. Colleges incorporating easy ways to find study groups and give Freshman the resources needed to acclimate to hard college classes could greatly benefit the success rate and happiness of students.

Best Study Habits

Form Study Groups

Making study groups and following through with them can be one of the most affective ways to learn and retain information. Study groups can be a good form of motivation and support. Talking over concepts with students in the same situation as you can help you take in and retain concepts that might otherwise be hard to understand.

Take Good Notes

Taking good notes in class helps students engage with what their teacher is showing and helps you make connections between topics. Creating mind maps with topics and subtopics helps many students. Other methods, such as creating charts to map out main takeaways are a good way to stay focused and recall information. Although this is not suggested for all students, some find that recording the class lectures and listening back later is the best way to remember important information. Handwriting notes instead of typing is known to help recall information, as it helps with comprehension and retention of information

Don't Procrastinate

Procrastinating is one of the easiest ways to fall behind in class. Avoid falling into this cycle by writing everything down the day it’s due, and making sure to check your calendar regularly. Setting breaks for yourself can help you accomplish attainable goals, and makes the task seem less daunting. Try breaking the task into chunks so the work load is easier, and reward yourself for finishing.

Research

Studies show that there is a clear and direct link between difficult classes and stress. For many college students, it is easy to fall in an academic trap of doing bad on a test or assignment, leading to feelings of discouragement, making their motivation fall quickly. This sort of pattern is what causes so many freshman to drop out of college early on, as they may feel discouraged from those first bad grades. Academic stress can turn into depression or anxiety, where students think they aren’t good enough, and may just give up entirely. With the right support, these cycles can be tackled and academics can become easier to handle.

Being a freshman in college can be a jarring transition. Going from having little responsibilities, to having total freedom over what you can do with your life can be intimidating. Having giant class sizes with little teacher-student interaction can also be scary at first. Some colleges have transition programs that help freshman to get their footing and find their place at their university. These kinds of programs are helpful to give students a sense of community and motivation in a completely new place.

Being in large college classroom setting makes it hard for students to make friends or study partners. A lot of the responsibility is placed on the student to complete their work on time, keep up with assignments, and figure out how to study. Large classes don’t encourage interaction, but reaching out and starting up a study group can make a large class feel much smaller and intimate. Finding people that you can discuss concepts with gives many students the support they need to do well in their class.
